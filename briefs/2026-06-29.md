## The Lead

**NVIDIA wires a self-improvement loop into physical robots — and they hit 99% on dexterous tasks.**  
**NVIDIA** released **ENPIRE**, a harness that gives real-world robots the same autonomous experiment-execute-evaluate loop coding agents run: an Environment module auto-resets and verifies, a Policy-Improvement module refines, a Rollout module evaluates across parallel robots, and an Evolution module where coding agents read logs, consult literature, and rewrite training code to fix failure modes. **Jack Clark** frames it as a 'taste of what it might look like for a superintelligence to attempt to use robots to instantiate itself in the physical world' — suggestive, not there yet, but the loop now closes without human judgment on tasks it can auto-score and auto-reset.  
- **Returns to scale:** Larger agent fleets (e.g. 8) reach higher-scoring solutions sooner, and multi-agent setups sometimes beat a single agent outright by exploring more of the solution space. **GPT-5.5** in Codex and **Opus 4.7** in Claude Code trade the top spot; **Kimi-2.6** lags.
- **The catch:** Task complexity is bounded by what can be *automatically* evaluated and reset — and fleet utilization drops as robot count scales because agents idle while reading logs and debugging. Hardware: paired YAM arms per station on a single RTX 5090.

## Research & Models

**The open-model frontier is no longer a Chinese duopoly — it's a long tail of niche makers.**  
**Nathan Lambert** argues the open ecosystem has diversified past the handful of (mostly Chinese) labs that dominated a year ago. He sorts the new entrants into pure model-makers (**Poolside, Arcee, Zyphra**), sovereign players (**Cohere, Mistral, Trillion**), Big Tech with upsell/GPU motives (**Qwen, Gemma, NVIDIA**), and product companies training narrow models (**JetBrains, Zed**). The thesis: more orgs ship a long tail of models while fewer chase the absolute open frontier — and attempts to ban the ecosystem are 'futile... unsafe and anti-freedom.'  
- **Licensing thaw:** **Cohere's** Command A+ (218B-A25B MoE, single-B200 at 4-bit) ships under Apache 2.0 after prior non-commercial releases; **Poolside's** Laguna-M.1 is Apache too, with open weights now their default. **NVIDIA's** Nemotron-3-Ultra-550B adopts the model-specific OpenMDW license over a custom one.
- **GLM-5.2 headline:** **zai-org's** GLM-5.2 is the issue's biggest release — 'genuinely usable for everyday work, not a huge regression compared to the best closed models,' though download numbers track in line with GLM-5.

## Agents & Tooling

**The way to deploy work agents in a company is to give every person their own — credential-free.**  
**NanoClaw's** founder says the killer enterprise pattern isn't an 'agent factory' but a personal **Claude**-style agent per employee — a 'second brain' you dump information into that builds an LLM-wiki of you, after the **Singapore Foreign Minister** publicly detailed his own Raspberry-Pi NanoClaw setup. The security model is the differentiator: each agent runs in its own container with **zero credentials in its environment**, all outbound requests proxied through a vault that injects credentials per access policy, plus human-in-the-loop approval (Slack approve/reject buttons) for actions like sending email.  
- **Minimal by design:** Built from scratch on the **Agent SDK** (vs. Open Claw's Pi) plus Vercel's Chat SDK for messaging — fewer dependencies after he found Open Claw logging all messages in plain text. 10-person team, 100+ companies in the deployment queue.
- **Memory is unsolved:** He runs a Karpathy-style LLM-wiki, not retrieval — 'I'm not sure retrieval based solutions are ideal for a personal assistant.' Duplicate-file sprawl still needs a background dedupe process nobody's built yet.

**A 3B Llama on-device matched Claude Sonnet on summarization and killed a dollar-a-day inference bill.**  
**Rachel Lee Neighbors (Arize)** lays out a 'prototype big, deploy small' method: prove a task on a frontier model, build a golden eval set, then walk up from the smallest local model until one is 'small and good enough' (her proposed term: the **SAGE** model). For her social-thread summarizer, **Llama 3.2 (3B, 2GB)** beat faster-but-dumber Qwen and slower **Gemma 4**, landing ~90% accuracy — closed to parity with **Claude Sonnet** via a few-shot prompt and post-processing, at zero marginal cost because inference runs on the user's device.  
- **Why small wins:** SLMs draw ~25% of an LLM's energy (task-specific models ~half that again), keep data on-device (no PII exposure), and beat the 4-second believability threshold for chat latency — P50 dropped to ~1s, P95 under Claude's.
- **Evals catch surprises:** Run on Arize's open-source **Phoenix**; the remaining 'factual' gap was just **Claude Opus** as judge favoring its own sibling Sonnet — 'crack them open' before trusting a score.
